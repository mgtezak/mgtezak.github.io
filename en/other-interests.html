Music

Apart from my ambitions in data science, I am deeply rooted in music. As the son of two musicians, I came into contact with music at a very early age. At 14, I founded my first band, and at 16, I began teaching guitar. Initially privately, and later at a music school. Over the years, I accompanied several dozen students aged from 5 to about 50 years old. I quickly realized that a teacher too has much to learn, e.g., how to create good teaching materials, how to communicate concepts and techniques in a pedagogically meaningful way, how to deal with very different personality types, and how to find the right balance between comfort and challenge. In addition, I always worked as a studio musician, concert musician, and even as a street musician. I played in numerous permanent or spontaneous groups and tried various musical genres: jazz-tinged singer-songwriter music, indie- and psychedelic rock, as well as R&B and Rap. Among others, I shared stages and studios with Bakery, Temple Haze, Júlia Pigali, Olmo, Emmae, Che Lingo, Alice Phoebe Lou und James Hersey.

Humanistic Volunteer Work

During my studies, I became a member of the SHBH (Secular Humanists at Berlin Universities) - a university group closely affiliated with the Giordano Bruno Foundation. Together, we organized public lectures and panel discussions with renowned public figures (e.g., Robin Hanson, Hamed Abdel Samad, Prof. Dr. Stefan Lorenz Sorgner, Prof. Dr. Wolfgang Nellen, Harald Ebner, Mario Brandenburg, Katrin Staffler, and many more). We engaged in productive debates and semi-public reading circles on topics such as religion, artificial intelligence, transhumanism, human rights, democracy, social networks, and journalism. In August 2019, I attended the summer forum of the Giordano Bruno Foundation and became a co-founder of a project group on the topic of debate culture in Germany. This was motivated by the increasing political polarization we particularly observed around us in the wake of the migration crisis and the intuition that it would be helpful to explore more generally when and why people retreat into rigid groupthink, motivated reasoning and filter bubbles, rather than engage each other with curiosity, good will, and a respect for factual accuracy. Two written publications emerged from our work (see below).
