I studied philosophy and economics at Humboldt-University Berlin, worked as a musician for more than a decade, and, in collaboration with the Giordano Bruno Foundation and three other authors, published a book on the topic of political discourse culture in Germany. In this regard, my academic as well as my professional background may appear somewhat unusual and perhaps not directly pointing towards a career in the tech industry. However, during my studies, I also discovered my enthusiasm for Python, which grew stronger as I began to delve into data analysis, visualization, and machine learning.

Data Science

Fascinated by the variety of intricate and sophisticated algorithms to help squeeze profound insights out of datasets, I decided to enroll in a three-month bootcamp at DataScientest. There, I gained experience with the following Python libraries and frameworks: Numpy, Pandas, Matplotlib, Seaborn, Bokeh, Scikit-Learn, XGBoost, Imblearn, Statsmodels, PySpark, TensorFlow, Keras, PyTorch, NLTK, SHAP, BeautifulSoup, Selenium, Pillow, OpenCV and Streamlit, as well as the database query language SQL. At the same time, I became familiar with the entire data science pipeline. From web scraping, data cleaning, exploratory analysis, hypothesis testing, and visualization to numerical encoding, dimension reduction, and feature engineering, up to the implementation of various machine learning algorithms: linear and logistic regression, decision trees and support vector machines, bagging and boosting algorithms, clustering methods, time series analyses, and even deep neural networks (dense, convolutional, and recurrent). Issues such as imbalanced datasets, missing entries, and the omnipresent tensions between underfitting and overfitting, accuracy and interpretability I got to know not just in the abstract - they were among the central challenges of my final project. After the bootcamp, I taught myself the fundamentals of how to use Docker, which ultimately enabled the web deployment of my app.

Data Structures & Algorithms

By now, I have solved a total of over 700 coding challenges on the platforms LeetCode, Advent of Code, Project Euler, and HackerRank. On one hand, because I have a passion for puzzle tasks, and on the other, because I have learned a tremendous amount from them, especially at the beginning. To solve tasks of this nature as efficiently as possible, it requires knowledge of common data structures and algorithms, as well as the ability to recognize which approach is appropriate for a given problem, and an understanding of the time- and space complexity of potential solutions. While university educated programmers covered all of this during their studies, I acquired this knowledge through self-study. Now, I have a thorough understanding of Heaps, Stacks, Queues, Graphs, Trees, and Hashmaps, and can implement Binary-Search, Depth-first-Search, Breadth-first-Search, Backtracking, Sliding-Window, and Dynamic-Programming algorithms.

Due to the fact that these platforms offer the opportunity to compare one's own code with that of thousands of others, I was able to develop a relatively good sense over time of what constitutes clean, clear, unambiguous, simple, and elegant (in short: pythonic) code. This also includes a certain familiarity with many of Python's own libraries such as dataclasses, collections, deque, itertools, functools, bisect, and regex.
